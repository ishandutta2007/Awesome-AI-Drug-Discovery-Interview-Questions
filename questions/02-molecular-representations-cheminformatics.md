# 2. Molecular Representations & Cheminformatics

How molecules are turned into something a model can actually learn from — a foundational skill that shapes every downstream modeling decision.

---

### Q: What is SMILES notation, and what are its main limitations as a representation for machine learning? 🟢

**Answer:**
SMILES (Simplified Molecular Input Line Entry System) is a text-based notation that encodes a molecule's structure as a linear string of characters representing atoms, bonds, branches, and rings.

Limitations for ML:
- **Non-uniqueness:** the same molecule can be written as multiple different valid SMILES strings depending on the starting atom and traversal order, which means a naive text model can treat chemically identical molecules as different inputs unless canonicalized.
- **Fragility:** small, arbitrary changes to a SMILES string (e.g., a single character edit) can produce an invalid string or a chemically very different molecule, meaning the string representation doesn't have a smooth relationship to chemical similarity — this makes naive text-based generative models prone to producing invalid molecules.
- **Loses explicit 3D/conformational information** — SMILES encodes 2D connectivity (topology) and stereochemistry annotations, but not 3D conformation, which matters for many property predictions (e.g., binding).

Because of these issues, many modern approaches use graph-based representations (treating atoms as nodes and bonds as edges) instead of, or alongside, SMILES strings, since molecular graphs more naturally reflect chemical structure and similarity.

### Q: What is a molecular fingerprint (e.g., ECFP/Morgan fingerprints), and how does it differ from a learned graph neural network embedding? 🟡

**Answer:**
A molecular fingerprint is a fixed-length bit vector encoding the presence/absence (or count) of specific substructural patterns in a molecule, typically generated by a deterministic algorithm — ECFP (Extended Connectivity Fingerprints, aka Morgan fingerprints) iteratively hash local atom neighborhoods of increasing radius into fixed-size bit positions.

Key differences from a learned GNN embedding:
- **Fingerprints are hand-designed and fixed** — the same molecule always produces the same fingerprint regardless of the downstream task, and the "features" (which substructures matter) aren't learned from data.
- **GNN embeddings are learned end-to-end for a specific task**, potentially capturing task-relevant structural patterns that a fixed fingerprint scheme might not emphasize, but requiring more data to train well and being less directly interpretable.
- **Fingerprints are fast, robust baselines** that often perform surprisingly competitively, especially on smaller datasets — a well-tuned classical model (e.g., random forest or gradient boosting) on ECFP fingerprints is a standard, strong baseline that any new GNN approach should be benchmarked against, not assumed to be automatically superior to.

### Q: What is Tanimoto similarity, and what is it used for in cheminformatics? 🟢

**Answer:**
Tanimoto similarity (a form of the Jaccard index applied to molecular fingerprints) measures the similarity between two molecules' fingerprint bit vectors as the size of their intersection divided by the size of their union — ranging from 0 (no shared bits/substructures) to 1 (identical fingerprints).

It's used for: clustering compound libraries, similarity-based virtual screening (finding molecules similar to a known active), assessing chemical diversity of a screening set, and — critically for ML evaluation — measuring how structurally similar a test set is to a training set, since high similarity between train and test compounds can make a model's performance look artificially strong (see also section 6 on evaluation).

### Q: What's the difference between 2D and 3D molecular descriptors, and when would you need 3D information for a modeling task? 🟡

**Answer:**
- **2D descriptors** are computed from a molecule's graph/connectivity alone (molecular weight, number of rings, topological polar surface area, 2D fingerprints) — fast to compute and don't require knowing the molecule's 3D shape.
- **3D descriptors** depend on a specific conformation (3D shape) of the molecule — e.g., 3D shape/pharmacophore features, or docking-based interaction features.

3D information matters most when the property being predicted is fundamentally about spatial fit — e.g., protein-ligand binding (docking scores, structure-based design), where the actual 3D shape and orientation of the molecule in a binding pocket is mechanistically relevant. The complication: molecules are flexible and can adopt many conformations, so "the" 3D structure isn't a single fixed thing — conformer generation and selection (which conformation to use, or whether to consider an ensemble) is itself a nontrivial modeling choice that affects downstream 3D-based predictions.

### Q: What is the concept of "chemical space," and why is it useful (and limited) as a mental model in drug discovery? 🟡

**Answer:**
Chemical space refers to the theoretical set of all possible small molecules (often estimated to be astronomically large, far exceeding what's ever been synthesized or could be experimentally tested). It's a useful mental model for reasoning about **coverage and novelty** — e.g., understanding that any real screening library or training dataset is an infinitesimally small, non-random sample of this space, which has major implications for how far you should trust a model to generalize to structurally novel regions.

Limitations: "distance" in chemical space (however it's measured — fingerprint similarity, learned embeddings) doesn't map cleanly onto differences in biological activity — two structurally similar molecules can have wildly different activity (an "activity cliff"), and two structurally dissimilar molecules can have similar activity. So chemical space is a useful but imperfect proxy, and shouldn't be treated as a literal, reliable coordinate system for biological behavior.

### Q: How would you deduplicate and standardize a compound dataset before using it for model training? 🟡

**Answer:**
- **Canonicalize structures** (using a standard canonical SMILES or InChI representation) so that different textual representations of the same molecule are recognized as identical rather than treated as distinct data points.
- **Standardize tautomers, charge states, and salt forms** — the "same" underlying molecule can appear in a dataset in different protonation states or as different salt forms, which should typically be normalized to a consistent representation unless the task specifically cares about that distinction.
- **Handle stereochemistry deliberately** — decide explicitly whether stereoisomers should be treated as distinct data points (often necessary, since stereoisomers can have very different biological activity) or merged, based on the task.
- **Check for and resolve duplicate measurements** of the same compound from different sources/assays, which may disagree — this requires a policy (e.g., averaging, flagging for review, or preferring a more reliable source) rather than silently keeping both or arbitrarily picking one.
